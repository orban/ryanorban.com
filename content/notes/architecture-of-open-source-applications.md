## Summary

The Architecture of Open Source Applications is a four-volume series where the developers who built major open-source projects explain how their software is structured and why. The project addresses a gap that most programming education leaves open: you can learn syntax and algorithms in isolation, but studying how real systems fit together requires access to experienced practitioners who designed them. AOSA makes that explicit — each chapter is written by someone who was in the room when the architectural decisions were made.

The series spans 75+ applications across four volumes. The first two cover general architecture (25 applications in Volume 1 including Git, Asterisk, Eclipse, Audacity; 24 more in Volume 2 including Firefox, nginx, GDB, PyPy). A third volume focuses entirely on performance optimization across 12 projects. The fourth, 500 Lines or Less, presents minimal implementations of key concepts — the constraints force architectural clarity. All volumes are available free online.

The series mirrors how architecture education works in other disciplines. Architects study historical buildings; engineers study landmark bridges. Software has the same need but fewer curated resources. AOSA is the closest thing to a canon of "here's how the field's most influential systems actually work."

## Key points

- 75+ real open-source applications explained by their authors — not reconstructed from code.
- Volume 3 (POSA) specifically focuses on performance tradeoffs, not just architecture.
- 500 Lines or Less forces architectural clarity through strict size constraints.
- All volumes available free online at aosabook.org.
- Covers Git, nginx, Firefox, GDB, PyPy, Eclipse, and many others.
