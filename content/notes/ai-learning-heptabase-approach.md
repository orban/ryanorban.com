## Summary

Alan Chan from Heptabase argues that AI's real value for learning isn't efficiency — it's access to harder material. The question isn't how to learn faster but "how to become capable of learning knowledge that is more complex, abstract, and challenging." AI should raise the ceiling of what you can engage with, not just lower the time cost.

The five-step approach centers on a digital whiteboard as a giant, borderless desk: (1) parse the full PDF with accurate extraction tools; (2) generate personalized study materials — translations, summaries, definitions tailored to your needs; (3) read and discuss with AI in full context visible on the whiteboard simultaneously; (4) take notes by rewriting in your own words, forcing judgment about what matters; (5) break accumulated notes into components and map their relationships visually.

The whiteboard metaphor does real work here: viewing extensive material simultaneously while querying an AI about specific parts maintains cognitive flow that tab-switching destroys. The simultaneous-visibility insight is practically useful for reading dense academic papers with an AI.

## Key points

- Key claim: AI should enable harder learning, not just faster same-difficulty learning.
- Whiteboard as simultaneous display — view source material and study notes without switching contexts.
- Full PDF parsing for accurate context, not search-based chunk retrieval.
- Taking notes in your own words forces judgment about importance and organization.
- Visualization step: break accumulated notes into components, map relationships.
- The Heptabase product provides the whiteboard substrate — spatial note organization.
