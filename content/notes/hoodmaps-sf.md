## Summary

Hoodmaps is a crowd-sourced neighborhood mapping tool where locals annotate city areas with informal descriptors — labels like hipsters, rich, suits, tourists, sketchy, and university that capture the social and cultural character of neighborhoods in a way that official neighborhood names and real estate boundaries don't. The San Francisco map is one of the most detailed, reflecting both the city's strong neighborhood identities and the engagement of its tech-adjacent user base.

The value of Hoodmaps over official maps is that it captures the *vibe* of an area rather than its administrative classification. SoMa covers a huge area officially; Hoodmaps annotations reveal which parts are tech offices, which are nightlife, which are homeless encampments, which are industrial. For someone new to a city, this kind of granular social geography is more useful than street names for understanding where they are and what to expect.

The project exists for most major cities worldwide, not just SF. Each map reflects the crowd-sourced opinions of whoever contributes — which means it can embed local biases and stereotypes, and popular/controversial areas tend to have more annotation density. It's crowd-sourced sociology, with all the noise that implies. Still, the aggregate picture is usually more accurate than any single person's mental map.

## Key points

- Crowd-sourced informal neighborhood labels: "hipsters," "suits," "tourists," "sketchy," etc.
- San Francisco version is particularly detailed — strong neighborhood identity + engaged contributors.
- Captures social/cultural geography that official neighborhood names and real estate boundaries miss.
- Available for most major cities worldwide.
- Crowd-sourced → can encode biases; popular areas have more annotation density.
- Useful for visitors, newcomers, and anyone trying to understand urban cultural geography quickly.
