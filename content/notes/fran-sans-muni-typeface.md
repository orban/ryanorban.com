## Summary

Fran Sans is a typeface created by designer Emily Sneddon based on the LCD destination displays used on San Francisco's Muni Breda Light Rail Vehicles. The original sign system was designed in 1999 by engineer Gary Wallberg at Trans-Lite, Inc. — built for sufficiency, not expression. Characters were constructed on a 3×5 grid using geometric modules (squares, quarter-circles, angled forms), resulting in letterforms that Sneddon describes as "mechanical and yet distinctly personal."

The research process involved visiting the SFMTA Electronics Shop, meeting the technician who maintained the signs, and eventually tracking down the original engineer. Sneddon reverse-engineered the letter anatomy from the modular components and built a complete typeface in three styles: Solid, Tile, and Panel. Some characters (Q, X) were never created in the originals because Muni destinations never needed them.

The project is partly archival: the Breda vehicles and their distinctive displays were being retired by 2025, replaced by newer trains. Fran Sans preserves the visual language of those signs as a usable typeface. This sits in a rich tradition of typography that comes from vernacular and functional sources — subway signage, highway lettering, industrial stencils — where constraints produced distinctive aesthetics that feel worth preserving.

## Key points

- Fran Sans derived from SF Muni Breda LRV LCD destination signs, designed in 1999 by Gary Wallberg.
- 3×5 grid system using geometric modules — designed for sufficiency, not style.
- Three styles: Solid, Tile, Panel. Missing Q and X (never needed for transit destinations).
- Research included archival work with SFMTA and the original sign engineer.
- Preservation project: Breda vehicles retired by 2025, the displays disappearing with them.
- Part of the tradition of vernacular typography — functional constraints producing distinctive letterforms.
