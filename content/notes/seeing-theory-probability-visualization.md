## Summary

Seeing Theory is an interactive educational project from Brown University that teaches probability and statistics through visual demonstration and hands-on simulation rather than formal mathematical exposition. The site covers six major areas: basic probability, compound probability, probability distributions, frequentist inference, Bayesian inference, and regression analysis.

The pedagogy is the key feature. Rather than reading about the law of large numbers, you flip a weighted coin, watch the sample mean converge, and feel the relationship between variance and sample size. Rather than memorizing the formula for Bayes' theorem, you drag a slider and watch the posterior distribution update in real time. This kind of learning builds intuition that equation-reading alone doesn't reliably produce.

Seeing Theory sits in a strong tradition of visual math education: 3Blue1Brown's video series, Khan Academy's interactive exercises, The Art of Problem Solving. What distinguishes it is the self-paced simulation model — you set parameters, observe outcomes, and develop hypotheses rather than following a guided walkthrough. It's closer to a laboratory than a lecture.

## Key points

- Six chapters: basic probability → compound probability → distributions → frequentist inference → Bayesian inference → regression.
- Interactive simulations: adjust coin weights, die distributions, sample sizes — immediate visual feedback.
- From Brown University — well-regarded in the probability and statistics education space.
- Covers the conceptual gap between mathematical definitions and statistical intuition.
- Particularly strong for Bayesian inference — watching posterior updates is more illuminating than reading the formula.
