## Summary

Folk Computer is an open-source project building a physical, tangible computing environment — "a computer for the real world." The name intentionally invokes folk art and folk music: improvised, practical, accessible tools made by and for communities, not specialists. The guiding question is whether computing can be as natural and learnable as folk crafts rather than requiring specialized training.

The project has backing from Ink & Switch (the research lab behind Ink & Switch work on local-first software and collaborative tools) and NYU ITP (Interactive Telecommunications Program, the graduate program where creative technologists make things). The community dimension is central — it develops through Discord, GitHub, newsletters, and partnerships rather than as a product.

Folk Computer sits in the tangible computing lineage alongside Bret Victor's Dynamicland project, which explored room-scale computing with paper and cameras. Both ask the same question: what does computing look like when it's embedded in physical reality rather than abstracted into screens? The practical examples — buttons, serial Bluetooth integration, OpenCV, QR scanning — suggest a hands-on physical + computation approach.

## Key points

- Tangible computing for the real world — physical objects as the computing interface.
- Community-driven development via GitHub, Discord, newsletters — not a product with a roadmap.
- Backed by Ink & Switch and NYU ITP — research credibility, creative technology tradition.
- Practical examples: GPIO buttons, Bluetooth, OpenCV, QR scanning — embedded in physical objects.
- Name invokes folk computing: accessible, improvised, community-made tools.
- In the lineage of Dynamicland by Bret Victor — room-scale, physical, collaborative computing.
