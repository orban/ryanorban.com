## Summary

The Soul of an Octopus by Sy Montgomery follows her time befriending octopuses at the New England Aquarium in Boston. It's structured as a memoir — four specific octopuses, each with their own personality, name, and relationship with the author — but uses that personal frame to explore questions about consciousness, intelligence, and what it means to have an inner life without a centralized brain.

Octopuses are a compelling case for thinking about distributed cognition: two-thirds of their neurons are in their arms, which can problem-solve semi-independently of the central brain. They have camera eyes that evolved completely separately from vertebrate eyes (convergent evolution), change color and texture for communication and camouflage, and live entirely alone (no social learning). Yet they demonstrate play behavior, individual personalities, and problem-solving that suggests rich cognitive life.

The book is often cited alongside Other Minds: The Octopus, the Sea, and the Deep Origins of Consciousness by Peter Godfrey-Smith as essential reading on cephalopod cognition. Together they represent a non-anthropocentric approach to understanding consciousness — using octopuses as a case study for thinking about minds that evolved on a completely different path from vertebrates.

## Key points

- Memoir about four octopuses at New England Aquarium — each named, each distinct personality.
- Uses personal narrative to explore consciousness, distributed cognition, and animal minds.
- Octopuses: 2/3 of neurons in arms; each arm semi-autonomous — a model of distributed intelligence.
- Independently evolved camera eyes (convergent evolution) — different path to similar capability.
- Pair with Other Minds (Peter Godfrey-Smith) for the philosophical treatment.
- One of the best general-audience books on animal cognition and the philosophy of mind.
